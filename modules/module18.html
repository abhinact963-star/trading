Trendlines

One straight line can tell you more about the market than any indicator. Learn how to draw them correctly, read them, and trade them.

What Is a Trendline?

A trendline is a straight line you draw on a chart to show which direction price is moving. In an uptrend, you draw it connecting the lows — the points where price dipped before bouncing back up. In a downtrend, you draw it connecting the highs — the points where price bounced up before falling again. The trendline shows you the "floor" or "ceiling" of the current move.

The most important rule: you need at least two points to draw a line, and a third touch to confirm it. Two points is just a guess. Three touches means the market has respected that line — and other traders are watching it too.

Uptrend Lines vs. Downtrend Lines

  • Uptrend line Drawn below price, connecting the lows. Acts as a moving support floor. Price keeps bouncing off it upward. As long as price stays above this line, the uptrend is alive.
  • Downtrend line Drawn above price, connecting the highs. Acts as a moving resistance ceiling. Price keeps hitting it and getting rejected. As long as price stays below this line, the downtrend is alive.

The Rule of Touches — When Is a Trendline Valid?

Not every line you can draw is a valid trendline. Here's the simple rule:

  • 2 touches — You have a line but no confirmation yet. Don't trade off it yet.
  • 3 touches — The line is now valid. The market has respected this level three times. This is where most traders start paying attention.
  • 4+ touches — A very strong trendline. The more touches, the more significant the eventual break will be.

Also: the touches should be spread out over time. Three touches bunched up in one day are less meaningful than three touches spread over three weeks.

Steep Trendlines Break Faster

The angle of a trendline tells you how sustainable it is. A very steep trendline (almost vertical) means price is rising too fast — it cannot maintain that pace and will almost certainly break sooner. A gentle, gradual trendline is more sustainable and tends to last longer. When a steep trendline breaks, price often slows to a more gradual uptrend rather than reversing completely.

Trendline Break — The Trend May Be Ending

When price finally closes below an uptrend line (or above a downtrend line), the trendline is broken. This is a warning sign that the trend may be over. But a single break is not enough — you want to see:

  • A candle that closes beyond the trendline (not just wicks through it)
  • Ideally on higher volume than usual
  • The break should be decisive — not a tiny nibble but a clear break

A trendline break does NOT automatically mean sell everything. It means: the old structure has changed — now look for new structure to form.

The Retest — Old Support Becomes New Resistance

After a trendline breaks, something very useful often happens. Price pulls back UP to test the broken trendline from below. Now the old support trendline acts as resistance — it flips its role. Traders who missed the break use this retest as an opportunity to enter short. This is one of the highest-probability setups in trading: a trendline break followed by a retest of the broken line.
