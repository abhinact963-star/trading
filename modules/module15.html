ICT, Wyckoff & Elliott Wave

Three advanced frameworks used by experienced traders. Each one helps you understand why price moves the way it does.

ICT — How the Market Manipulates Retail Traders

ICT (Inner Circle Trader) is a framework that says large players deliberately manipulate price to trick small traders before making the real move. Here are the key ideas:

  • Judas Swing — At the start of the trading session, price makes a fake move in the wrong direction. Small traders enter. Then the real move begins in the opposite direction, stopping them out.
  • Power of Three — Every day has a pattern: a quiet morning build-up, a false move that traps traders, then the real sustained move. Identifying which phase you're in is the key.
  • Kill Zones — The best setups usually appear in specific time windows: around the London open (2:30 PM IST) and the New York open (7 PM IST). That's when the biggest orders enter.
  • Liquidity Raid — Price sweeps above a recent high (taking out stop-losses), then reverses hard in the other direction.

Wyckoff — How Big Money Secretly Builds and Unloads Positions

Richard Wyckoff studied how large professional traders operate. Here is the pattern he found:

  • Accumulation — A big player wants to buy millions of shares. If they buy all at once, price shoots up and they pay too much. So they buy quietly over weeks while price moves sideways. Nobody notices. Then they start the rally.
  • Distribution — Same idea in reverse. Big players sell slowly at a high level while price looks like it's "consolidating." Then the crash begins.
  • Spring — Just before the real rally, price dips below the accumulation range for a moment. This shakes out the last weak sellers (who panic-sell), then the big move up begins.
  • Upthrust — Just before the real drop, price briefly spikes above the distribution range. This tricks the last buyers into entering, then the crash begins.

Elliott Wave — Markets Move in Waves

Ralph Elliott noticed that markets move in a consistent repeating pattern. There are 5 waves in the direction of the main trend, then 3 waves correcting it. Waves 1, 3, and 5 move with the trend. Waves 2 and 4 are pullbacks. Wave 3 is usually the biggest and fastest. After wave 5, the market corrects in 3 waves (called A, B, C). This pattern reflects how crowd psychology evolves: quiet optimism in wave 1, peak excitement at wave 5, then fear in the correction.
