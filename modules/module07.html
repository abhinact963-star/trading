Supply & Demand

Price zones where big buyers or sellers were active. When price comes back to these zones, something usually happens.

Demand Zones — Where Buyers Are Still Waiting

Imagine a huge buyer wanted to buy 10 lakh shares of a stock. He couldn't buy them all at once — too obvious. So he placed buy orders across a price range. Price shot up from that area because of all his buying. A demand zone is that price range. When price comes back down to it, his remaining orders may still be there — and price bounces again.

Animation — Price entering a demand zone and bouncing

Supply Zones — Where Sellers Are Still Waiting

Same idea but reversed. A big seller placed sell orders across a price range. Price dropped sharply from there. A supply zone is that range. When price rises back into it, the remaining sell orders may push it back down again. Supply zones are ceilings. Demand zones are floors.

Animation — Price entering a supply zone and rejecting

Fresh vs. Used Zones

A zone is fresh if price hasn't come back to it since it was created. Fresh zones are stronger — the big orders are likely still there. Once price enters a zone and reacts, the zone gets weaker. If price passes straight through a zone without reacting, the zone is now invalid (the orders have been filled) and should be ignored.
