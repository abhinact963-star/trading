Algorithmic Trading & Backtesting

Can you turn a trading idea into a system? Here's how to test it honestly — and avoid the traps that fool almost everyone.

What Is Backtesting?

Backtesting means taking your strategy's rules and running them against old price data to see how they would have performed. For example: "Every time the 20-day MA crossed above the 50-day MA, I would buy. How would that have done over 5 years?" You apply this to historical data and look at the results. It's a useful test — but NOT a guarantee the strategy will work going forward.

Overfitting — Why Most Backtests Lie

This is the trap everyone falls into. You test a strategy. It fails. You adjust settings. Test again. Still fails. Adjust again. After many adjustments, it finally "works perfectly" on old data. But this is worthless. You haven't found a real edge — you've just memorised past events. This is called overfitting. A strategy that works on old data but fails on new data is not a strategy — it's just pattern-matching noise. The fix: fewer adjustments, bigger sample of trades.

Walk-Forward Testing — The Only Honest Test

Split your historical data into two parts. Use Part 1 to build and set up your strategy. Then, without changing anything at all, run it on Part 2 — data the strategy has never seen. If it still performs reasonably well on Part 2, you may have a real edge. If it collapses, it was overfitting and you need to start again.

Slippage — Real Trading Is Never Perfect

In a backtest, every trade fills at exactly the price you wanted. In real trading, that almost never happens. You want to buy at ₹500 and get filled at ₹501. This small difference is called slippage. Over hundreds of trades, small slippage adds up and can turn a "profitable" backtest into a real-world loser. Always build in realistic slippage and brokerage costs before trusting any backtest.

Total Risk Across All Trades

Imagine you have 5 trades open at the same time and all 5 go wrong at once. How bad is the damage? This is why you need to cap your total open risk at all times. Example rule: never have more than 5% of your account at risk across ALL open trades simultaneously. Even a great strategy cannot protect you if your sizing is too large and everything goes wrong together.
